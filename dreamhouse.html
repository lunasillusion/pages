The Nest

A Hand-Sculpted Home in the Mountains
Simply Asheville
The Vision

Not a big space.
The right space.

"I want to live inside something that was made — not assembled. A space that breathes, that holds warmth, that smells like the earth it came from. I've stayed in buildings like this on four continents and I've always felt the same thing: I'm home."

What follows is a living document. It will grow as the vision clarifies. The through-line running through every space that has ever felt right: organic curves, natural materials, abundant light, connection to the outside, handmade details, and the complete absence of hard industrial surfaces.

This is not a style choice. It is a philosophy. And it is entirely buildable.

Structure & Form

The hybrid approach

A round or organically shaped structure. The skeleton handles code compliance and structural integrity; everything layered within and around it carries the soul of the building.

The Skeleton

A timber frame or prefab round shell — potentially a Smiling Woods Yurts kit as the structural spine — engineered to code, permitted conventionally. Local Appalachian hardwood posts as primary interior vertical elements, sourced from regional sawyers, natural character preserved.

The Walls

Hempcrete cast in place around the timber frame using flexible formwork to achieve rounded corners and organic curves. Cob used for sculptural detail elements — arches, niches, window surrounds, built-in seating. The two materials are natural partners.

The Surface

Earthen or lime plaster finish throughout — interior and exterior. No paint required; tinted naturally through the plaster itself. Mosaic work pressed directly into soft walls. The building becomes a slow art project rather than a construction site.

The Roof

Generous overhangs (minimum 2–3 ft) to protect earthen walls from Asheville's rainfall. Metal roofing or natural materials depending on form. A skylight or dome element for the quality of light that makes circular spaces feel alive.

Materials Palette

What the walls
are made of

Every material chosen for sensory quality, ecological integrity, and regional availability.

Hempcrete
Walls · Insulation · Mass

Hemp hurd + lime binder. Thermally massive, hygroscopic, mold-resistant. Can be sculpted into curves. Carbon-negative. Sourced regionally from the Carolinas.

Cob
Sculptural Elements

Clay, sand, straw. Hand-formed for arches, niches, benches, window surrounds, fireplace surround. The material that makes a house feel hand-made.

Cork
Flooring · Detail Work

Soft underfoot, warm, natural. Ideal for bedroom floors. Can be used in geometric or decorative patterns. Pairs beautifully with hempcrete walls — both deeply natural, both tactile.

Local Timber
Frame · Interior Posts · Beams

Appalachian hardwood from regional sawyers. Post-Helene downed timber being milled locally. Natural character — not dimensioned box-store lumber.

Earthen & Lime Plaster
All Surfaces · Finish

Interior and exterior. Naturally tinted — no paint needed, no siding needed. Tadelakt (polished lime) for wet areas. The surface that makes a cob house glow.

Local Stone
Foundation · Accent

Appalachian fieldstone for rubble trench foundation, outdoor walls, pathway edging. Abundant on most WNC parcels. Zero transport cost if on-site.

Spatial Design

How the space
is organized

One large open volume as the heart. Minimal interior walls. Wet areas clustered together. The principle: maximum openness, minimum division.

The Main Space

Large, open, undivided. Living, cooking, eating as one continuous environment. Curved walls, natural light from dome or skylight, rocket mass heater as the thermal and visual center of the home.

The Kitchen

Integrated into the main space. Cob or hempcrete counters. Earthen plaster walls. No upper cabinets — open shelving with natural wood. Propane cooking.

The Bathroom

Open or semi-open concept — no full enclosure required. Tadelakt-finished wet surfaces (polished lime plaster, naturally waterproof). Shower at the edge of living space, as experienced in Costa Rica. A mosaic feature wall.

Sleeping

The one space that closes — a soft partition or low wall, not a full room. Cork floors for warmth underfoot. An abundance of natural light in the morning.

The Garden

Not separate from the house — continuous with it. Kitchen garden steps from the door. Fruit trees planted in year one. Perennial beds that mature over the five years of the build. The land as a room.

Guest · Ceremony

A separate structure — the yurt in its second life, or a small cob outbuilding. Its own space, its own entrance, its own energy. This comes later.

Off-Grid Systems

Self-sufficient
by design

The goal is not roughing it — it is genuine independence. Every system chosen for simplicity, longevity, and minimal maintenance.

  • Solar + Battery Quality lithium battery bank, 3–4 panels, inverter. Handles all daily needs including a mini-split. Installed cost $6,000–12,000. Not a compromise — genuinely comfortable off-grid power.
  • Water Well drilled as first infrastructure investment (shared with eventual house). Cistern as temporary bridge. Rainwater collection as supplementary system — barrels integrated architecturally, not as an afterthought.
  • Heating Rocket mass heater as primary heat source — cob-built, thermally massive, burns a fraction of the wood a conventional stove uses. The most natural building technology available. Wood stove as backup.
  • Sanitation Composting toilet (Nature's Head or equivalent) during yurt phase. Full septic installed when house is built. Gray water dispersed through planted leach field.
  • Propane Tank + line for cooking and on-demand hot water. Simple, reliable, minimal.
  • Rain Barrels Integrated into roof drainage. For garden irrigation primarily. Simple, inexpensive, architecturally considered.
Phase One

Living on the land
while it becomes home

A quality yurt — 24' to 30' diameter, four-season insulation package, proper deck — placed on the land while the house takes shape. Not a temporary situation. A real home, finished with intention from the first day.

The yurt sets up the infrastructure the house will inherit: the well, the driveway, the solar, the site clearing. Nothing is wasted. The garden begins in year one. The land is being known and loved while the building is being designed.

In its second life: a guest space, a ceremony space, an Airbnb. Finished with the same natural sensibility as the house — earthen plaster over the liner, local wood details, natural textiles.

Yurt Candidates

Pacific Yurts
Gold standard · Oregon

The most proven four-season yurt manufacturer in the US. Snow-load rated for WNC. 10–15 year cover lifespan. Strong resale market.

Smiling Woods Yurts
Round home kits · Washington State

Wood-walled, barn-roofed round home kits. Permitted as real structures. Cedar siding, triple-pane skylights, metal roof. 300–1,200+ sq ft. A more permanent option that bridges yurt and house.

Colorado Yurt Company
Four-season · Colorado

Excellent four-season specs. Good insulation package options. Strong customer support and builder community.
